What was being used

Current emergency banner design

Current survey banner on mobile

The current design:

  • appears as soon as the page loads
  • is not mobile friendly
  • is not accessible

Previous research

Medicines team

Medicines used a similar survey banner design. They originally had it positioned at the top of the page and were getting approx. 4 users a day. They moved it to the bottom of the page and started to get approx. 40 users per day.

Survey analysis

Previous surveys, using the current design, included the question “What could be improved on this page?”.

2% of users who answered the question, told us they hadn’t had a chance to look/read the page before being prompted to take part in a survey.

Answers included:

  • “Survey came up before I could read the article”
  • “I haven't read up on it yet. Survey comes up too soon.”
  • “Ask me this survey after I’ve looked for what i want xx”

User need

As a user, I would like to provide feedback on the page / service / journey I'm using, so that the service / team / product working on the thing can improve it to better suit my needs.

Design and research

Early work / thinking

Banner table

To help with the design, we created a table to determine the criteria for each banner, and thought about whether each banner should:

  • appear at the start or end of the users’ journey?
  • be passive or prominent?
  • be temporary or permanent?

We decided the survey banner should:

  • appear after the users’ journey – so we don’t annoy them (too much)
  • be prominent – so they notice it
  • be able to close (dismiss) it

Banner gate

Initial thinking was to make the survey banner bright (nhs warm yellow), so it would grab the user’s attention.

The problem was, all the banners clashed aka Banner Gate. It was all a bit of a mess. No hierarchy.

Banner gate

Also, the colour contrast of the links did not meet AA accessibility guidelines, making it hard for users to see.

What does gov do?

gov.uk survey banner

GOV.UK’s survey banner:

  • is positioned at the top of the page
  • has 2 actions, take survey and close survey and
  • uses a descriptive heading h2 and link text.

First iteration

Hypothesis

If we show the survey banner on the page after x seconds
Then users are less likely to get annoyed
Because they have had time to look at the page.

If we use a text link instead of a button
Then the quality of the responses will improve
Because the user will have a better understanding of the task, they are about to do.

If we use a light (white) background
Then the link to survey will be more visible
Because of the increased colour contrast and affordance.

See the banner in context (demo page)

Research findings

We are going to test the survey banner on the live website because it’s:

  • the only way to test at scale whether people are clicking on it
  • low risk

Accessibility testing

We did test the survey banner with a fully-blind person, with whom found one issue.

When the banner appeared on the page after 5 seconds, VoiceOver on iOS proceeded to read aloud the banner content, but the focus remained where it was. Which confused the user as they tried tabbing onto the banner.

Other than the issue above, the interaction with the banner tested well. The user understood its purpose and what actions to take.

The accessibility fix: The reason for VoiceOver reading the banner aloud was because the banner included an ARIA role of alert (role=”alert”). Once we removed the role, the issue was solved.

Accessibility

The survey banner now:

  • exceeds AAA colour contrast guidelines with its text colour
  • exceeds AA colour contrast guidelines with its link colour (just short of AAA)
  • has a ‘Close’ action is button and not a link. Find out more about accessible links and buttons
  • includes hidden text within the ‘Close’ button to give screen readers context, for example: ‘Close feedback invite’
  • has a descriptive heading (h2)
  • includes link text that describes its purpose and destination.

Usage (rules)

  • Does not appear until the user has agreed to use the appropriate cookies
  • Does not appear when the emergency banner is in use
  • Include a descriptive heading (h2)
  • Make the link text describe its purpose and destination
  • Add hidden text to the close button to give screen readers context.

Going forward

What is success

We calculated a baseline from the current banners’ performance.

Current banners performance (1-week period, 9th-16th Aug 2018):

  • Total users: 3,971,488 (according to Webtrends)
  • Clicks to survey: 76,173 (1.91%)
  • Survey completes: 25,593 (33% of users who clicked (0.64% of total users))

The target for new banner:

  • Clicks to survey: 4%
  • Survey completes: 50% of users who clicked (2% of total users)

How we measure success

We will use Webtrends (or Adobe Analytics) and User Zoom to analyse the banners performance.
